Mika Lambert

PhD student in Astronomy and Astrophysics at the University of California Santa Cruz

Hello! I'm Mika Lambert, currently a first-year PhD student at the University of California Santa Cruz (go banana slugs!)

My research interests include galaxy evolution and formation, specifically the internal structures of the Milky Way and dwarf galaxies. I also enjoy learning about stellar evolution and exoplanets!

I was born in Boston, MA, raised in Phoenix, AZ, and obtained a B.S. in astronomy at the University of Arizona. I owe my curiosity for the cosmos to my high school physics teacher and the 1997 movie 'Contact'. I hope to inspire the next generation of students to pursue STEM fields and make science in higher education more accessible through outreach programs!

Current Research

My current research project is investigating the inhomogeneity of vertical structure of the Milky Way. Previous studies have used K and M dwarf stars to probe the region 2 kpc above and below the galactic plane in the local region. We want to go beyond this region to see if the inhomogeneity continues further out.

Previous Research

Systematically Measuring Ultra-Diffuse Galaxies (SMUDGes). VI. Nuclear Star Clusters
submitted to AJ August 6, 2023 (no link yet)

I developed analysis software to identify nucleated low surface brightness galaxies, specifically ultra-diffuse galaxies (UDGs), and measure the properties of their nuclear sources. The goal was to measure the incidence of nuclear clusters in these galaxies and to uncover any galaxy properties that led to an increased incidence rate. The properties of the nuclear core will also be used to constrain models for the formation and evolution of these cores.

TOI-5375: An Object at the Hydrogen Burning Limit Orbiting an Early M-type Star
published to AJ April 28, 2023

I followed up on TESS objects of interests (TOIs) with ground-based high-resolution spectroscopy from HPF to measure radial velocities and characterize the host star and exoplanet. I specifically worked on characterizing a system, labeled TOI-5375. I modeled the orbital parameters, mass, and radius of the target, and concluded the companion is a very low-mass star at the hydrogen-burning limit.
